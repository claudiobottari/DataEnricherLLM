The newest models of quantum computers include hybrid quantum computing architectures. These architectures involve the integration of classical and quantum processes to solve problems. Microsoft has developed a taxonomy to understand each architecture and its benefits. 

- Batch quantum computing: Local clients define circuits and submit them as jobs to the quantum processing unit (QPU). Batching multiple circuits into one job allows for faster execution of multiple jobs. This approach is suitable for problems like Shor's algorithm and simple quantum phase estimation.

- Interactive quantum computing: The client compute resource is moved to the cloud, resulting in lower latency. The quantum circuit can be executed with different parameters, and jobs can be grouped logically into sessions for prioritization. This approach is useful for problems like Variational Quantum Eigensolvers (VQE) and Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithms (QAOA).

- Integrated quantum computing: The classical and quantum architectures are tightly coupled, allowing classical computations to be performed while qubits are coherent. This enables the use of common programming constructs and real-time adaptation to the quantum processing unit. This model is suitable for scenarios like adaptive phase estimation and machine learning.

- Distributed quantum computing: Classical computation works alongside logical qubits with long coherence time. This architecture enables complex and distributed computation across heterogenous cloud resources. It can be used to solve problems like evaluation of full catalytic reactions, carbon capture, and drug discovery.

These new models of quantum computers offer different capabilities and can be used for various types of problems.
